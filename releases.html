Solo Compositions

r lego

for MIDI-controlled organ · 7 Feb 2026, Hallgrímskirkja, Reykjavík · ca. 4'

info

In 2025 I got asked to do a midi piece for the Hallgrímskirkja organ. I got to do a few experiments in the church and as usual I got really frustrated — it all, for lack of a better description, sounded like an organ. I tried to get some inspiration from Ligeti's organ pieces but figured out a lot of the beautiful textures he achieves stem from the fact that he had control of the wind pressure, which I had no control of.

I made a few generators in Max focusing on the highest and lowest registers of the organ, using only 2 stops — mostly stops closest to sine waves. The length of the notes was also, most of the time, not much longer than 20ms.

I had one object generate clouds of notes through a chaotic process and another generating clusters with semi-stable rhythm. I created parameters for the density of the process, speed, and how large a range of notes to pull from, as well as where on the register. I also had control of the stops in/out messages, so I could use these same processes on the stops, which created faint mechanical wooden sounds. With these rapid, super-short tones I managed to get to a point where I was fairly happy with the sound.

stykki1

for electric guitar, cello, and piano · commissioned by K!art ensamble · 13 Aug 2022, Mengi, Reykjavík · ca. 6–12'

info

In 2022 I made a piece for the Danish ensemble K!ART, performed in Mengi. The piece was an attempt to make a modular score. I made a lot of experiments on my guitar and a piano I had access to. I always like sounds where the production is not easily recognized, so with instrumental music I have often been frustrated by distinct instrumental sound and I try my best to find new ways of instrumental sound production.

For this I tuned all the guitar strings so low that you can barely hear a stable pitch. From that I found a lot of textures that excited me. For the piano I just put Blu-tack on all the strings to dampen the traditional overtones and tried to distribute it diagonally for overtone variations.

From these experiments I created sound blocks and notated them similar to some scores by Lachenmann. I left the speed of the blocks open, as well as allowing the instrumentalists to start wherever in the block, with freedom to read it forward and backwards. I also left it up to the players to move between blocks freely. Ideally the players should study the blocks and remember them to go smoothly from one to the other — creating textures I did not imagine.
